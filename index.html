Principles

A few principles I find myself returning to, regardless of the problem.

  • Ship things that work. Scope it small enough to learn. Build it solid enough to scale.
  • Think in patterns, not solutions. A well-structured solution scales to users and use cases you didn't design for.
  • Define what good looks like. Standards you can point to outlast the ones you carry in your head.
  • Build to find clarity. A rough prototype surfaces more than a long discussion, and nothing aligns a room faster than something you can react to.

Current working process

A toolkit developed over 15 years, shaped more recently by AI. What I reach for depends on the problem.

  1. Understand the domain Start by identifying the gaps. The goal is to build enough knowledge to ask the right questions. AI has made it easier to start, particularly for domain knowledge, competitive context and early POVs.
  2. Abstract the system Understand what's underneath the problem: data models, constraints, patterns. Wireframes or Figma for spatial relationships, a doc when the work is naming entities and establishing taxonomy.
  3. Shape a prototype Once the system makes sense, start building. Usually in Claude Code nowadays. It clarifies thinking and produces something concrete to share with a team.
  4. Calibrate fidelity to the collaboration The output depends on my role and how the team is working together. Sometimes detailed Figma, sometimes rough screens polished later with engineering.
  5. Test a point of view, not an open question A small, considered MVP to real users beats testing a prototype if the timeline allows. When testing early, it's to validate a direction not discover one. It's something I feel strongly about and spend a lot of time developing in my team.
